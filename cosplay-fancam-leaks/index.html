Notes from the curator · § 01

A vault, not a feed.

This vault holds the cosplay fancam cuts I kept after eight years of filtering convention floors. Most fancam archives online are dump sites — every angle anyone ever shot, sorted by upload date, signed by nobody. This isn't that. Every plate in this vault was hand-pulled from a much larger pool, watched start to finish, and only then signed off into the main archive.

The frame I work in: a fancam isn't a clip, it's a document. A clip can be anything that pleases the algorithm for thirty seconds. A document has to survive in twenty years. The cuts in this vault are picked for the second.

What gets in

There's no automated criteria. The closest I get to a rule is this: would I show this plate to a friend in five years? If the answer is yes, it's in. If it's "maybe" or "the angles are good but…", it doesn't make it. Most of the work I cut fails on one of three things:

  • Multi-cam discipline. Single-angle floor shots from someone holding a phone are noise. Multi-cam edits — the kind RY.Creator and Cosforce03 publish — are documents. Two angles, color matched, audio synced. That's the floor.
  • Subject control. A creator running their own fancam booth, posing for the multi-cam team, in a costume they fitted themselves. That's a plate. Random sneak shots taken without consent never make it into this vault. Period.
  • Print quality. 8K masters from Cosforce03, Z8 24-70 with proper lighting, A7iv multi-rig setups — those clear the bar. Phone shots from the floor under mall food court LED don't.

Why no algorithm feed

If you're here from Google, you searched something specific. The platform won't try to redirect you sideways. There's no "you might also like" carousel pushing ten clips you didn't ask for. The hot ranking lists five cuts a week, the grid shows the rest, and the order is mine — not a recommender. I wrote the long version of why elsewhere.

This is on purpose. Algorithm feeds optimize for time-on-platform, not value-per-cut. They incentivize fast-turn content and reward creators who can produce twenty clips a week. The result is a flood of mediocre work that drowns out the actual archive. We're trying the opposite: slow archive, fast filter. If you spend forty minutes here, you should leave with a memory of something specific — a plate you'll pull up again next month — not a vague sense of having scrolled through a hundred things.

The convention scene · 2018 → 2026

The fancam scene moved fast in this decade. 2018 was Korean cosplay community boards, 720p uploads, hand-held DSLR. 2020 hit pause on the convention floor and the scene shifted to studio shoots and home setups — the early Sweet Maid drops are from this era. 2022-2024 was Cosforce03 era proper: multi-day events with verified creator booths, multi-cam setups paid for by the booth, professional lighting directors on rotation.

What's in this vault reflects that timeline. The earliest cut is from 2019, the newest is last Thursday's drop. Each plate carries the year and creator in the description so you can watch the scene evolving as you scroll. Three arcs to follow:

  • Nazo Eirian / Sweet Maid — six drops across 2023-2024, all 8K, all Cosforce03 productions. The interactive booth runs are the high-water mark for fancam staging in the decade.
  • Kadorin Hitomi — Taiwan/Japan convention floor across 2024-2026. Notable for the 8K FF48 floor pieces and the NIKKE costume cycle. Pairs naturally with the Japan cosplay archive.
  • Halloween 8K (Cosforce03) — the most-replayed plate in this vault, top of the hot ranking three weeks running. The maid theme played hard into a high-end multi-cam setup.

For the obsessive — what to look for

When you've watched a few hundred fancams, the markers that separate the documented from the noise start to surface. If you're new to this vault, here's what to listen for:

  • Color match across cameras. If the colorist did the work, the wide and the close-up should feel like the same room. Cheap fancams have one camera blue and one camera orange.
  • Audio sync on the cut. Bad fancams cut to a new angle and the audio drifts a frame or two — your brain registers it before your eyes do. Good ones, you don't notice the cut until you look for it.
  • Stable lighting. Booth fancams from a fixed multi-cam rig have stable light. Sneaky floor shots have the light shifting as the operator walks. The vault is heavily biased toward the first.
  • The third camera. Real productions have a third unit — usually a slider or top-down — that shows up for a few seconds and disappears. When you see it, you're watching a plate from a real production.
